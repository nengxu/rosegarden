3 Recording

Now that you've configured your Studio, you're probably eager to create some segments that contain something interesting. In this chapter, I discuss different ways of recording live performances with Rosegarden. My focus for the moment still lies with manipulating data on the main window.

3.1 Three Ways to Record

There are three different ways to record with Rosegarden. If you come from a MIDI background, the most obvious thing to do is plug in your MIDI keyboard and hit the record button to record a MIDI segment in the currently-selected track. If audio is your aim, you'll want to plug something into an input port on your soundcard, set your mixer recording source and get to work. On the other hand, if you have all the keyboard skill of a paraplegic chipmunk, like I do, you might be more interested in the step recording feature described in chapter 7.

3.1.1 Connecting a Track

Before you record, you might need to assign and output device to the selected track. If you're recording MIDI, then any MIDI device will do. Rosegarden cannot yet handle multiple, concurrent MIDI inputs, so the input you wish to use for recording should already have been connected while you were setting up your Studio. (See Chapter 3 for more details.) If you're recording audio, you'll need to tell Rosegarden that the currently selected track should contain audio segments. Do this by directing its connection to one of the Jack channels.


Click on one of the track labels and hold the button down for a moment.

You should be presented with a series of nested menus that allow you to choose the MIDI (or Jack) device and channel for the track.



3.1.2 What Do Those Black Boxes Mean?

While it's not immediately relevant to our discussion of recording, this is a convenient place to talk about the odd little boxes you might have noticed in the menus above.

The column of small boxes to the left of the channel provide a concise way of showing information about the status of these connections. In the examples above, you might have noticed that they were in several different states. That's because I have connections configured in my Studio for devices that aren't currently available, because the associated softsynths aren't running, and because some of my external MIDI, and some of my Jack channels weren't assigned to anything. (See Chapter 7 for more information about running softsynths in conjunction with Rosegarden.)

These boxes convey the following information:

This instrument or device isn't very useful to you right now. It is is not in use by a track, and it is not connected to any active output path.

This instrument or device could provide a valid output path, and all you need to do from here is attach it to a track and begin making use of it. It is connected to an active output path, but it is not being used by any track.

A track or tracks are connected to this device, but this is not currently a valid output path, and the events on any tracks that are connected don't have anywhere to go. This instrument or device is in use by a track, but is not connected to an active output path.

This instrument or device provides a valid output path, and that path is in use by at least one track. It has a valid connection to an output path, and that connection is in use by a track.

In addition, the instrument in use by the current track will have an extra black outline around either a black filled or black outlined box, depending on whether or not the path is connected to a valid output:

The active track is connected to an output that is also connected. If you play this track, you can expect the device you've hooked up to make some noise.

The active track is connected to an output that is not connected. This track, on the other hand, won't make any noise until you assign a different device to it or turn on the device that isn't currently working.
